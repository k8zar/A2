Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Here, we investigated U.S. Census data from 2020 on housing demographics from Boston-area zip codes. We were able to correlate corporate-ownership rate and location, finding that there was a higher rate of corporate ownership downtown and in waterfront neighborhoods relative to inland neighborhoods further from the downtown. We were also able to see decreased vacancies with higher owner-occupancy rates, though the correlation we observe is certainly not causation. From analysis of racial demographics by neighborhood, we find Boston to be predominantly white. Neighborhoods away from the downtown and college campus areas tend to be more diverse, with Dorchester being the most diverse. We postulate this could be because of the low corporate ownership rate and high housing units in the neighborhood, but without additional data it is difficult to make strong claims as to why this may be.

In this analysis, little was done to transform the data as provided from the csv file itself. In the case of the map data, some zip codes were excluded on account of their being so spatially localized. In other plots, we chose to use data from a single Dorchester zip code as we suspected the data was replicated across all four zip codes, as opposed to being specific to each area. I did not perform additional calculations on the dataset in the plots I made, mostly because the dataset was limited, which made it very diffcult to ask specific questions. Many of the visualizations I chose to include for this assignment are mean to look for trends, rather than highlight known existing truths. The dataset promised a time axis, however I discovered quickly that the data from 2020 was replicated to give the appearance of a time axis, but was in actuality all from a single year. Had this axis existed, faceting the data and performing more calculations on the existing categories could have been more relevant than I found it to be with this dataset alone.

I also note that many of the plots were in similar forms (bar chart, map, or bubble chart). The continuity of chart across multiple analysis questions was mostly done to be able to more faithfully compare trends across very different facets of the dataset. Looking at demographics by neighborhood is already restrictive of an aspect of the data, and being able to compare lengths or sizes by neighborhood category across different lenses offered by this dataset I found helpful, which is why I chose these visualizations. I recognize that the many colors used to further visually encode the data within the visualizations is overall more distracting than demonstrative of trends, however I found it incredibly useful for understanding the data by neighborhood. If the bar charts had yielded clearer trends, it could have made sense to further facet and downselect the data to investigate, however with this particular dataset my bar charts prompted me to do the opposite, due to a lack of apparent trend. Instead I found mapping the data geo-spatially and looking at housing type by neighborhood the most insightful.
